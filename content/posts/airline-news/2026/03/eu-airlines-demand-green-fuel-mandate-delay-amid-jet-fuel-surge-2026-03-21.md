# EU Airlines Demand Green Fuel Mandate Delay Amid Jet Fuel Surge

> **2026-03-21** — Major airlines operating from **Paris (CDG/ORY)**, **London (LHR/LGW)**, and **Rome (FCO)** have formally petitioned the European Union to postpone mandatory sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) blending requirements, citing a dramatic surge in jet fuel costs linked to escalating geopolitical tensions in Iran. Carriers warn that combined regulatory pressure and fuel market volatility could force significant ticket price increases across transatlantic and European routes within weeks.

## Key Developments

- **Petition Filed:** Major European carriers from Paris, London, and Rome submitted formal requests to delay EU SAF mandates effective April 1, 2026
- **Fuel Cost Spike:** Jet fuel prices have doubled from baseline levels following recent Iran conflict developments, creating supply chain disruption
- **Competitive Concern:** Airlines argue that SAF blending mandates will further increase operating costs when fuel surcharges are already unsustainable
- **Timeline Pressure:** EU regulatory deadline remains fixed at April 1, 2026, despite industry objections
- **Passenger Impact:** Carriers warn of imminent ticket price increases averaging 12-18% on affected routes

## Full Coverage: What We Know

**Air France**, **British Airways**, **Ryanair**, **easyJet**, and **Alitalia** (among others) jointly appealed to EU Transport Commissioner on March 20, 2026, requesting a 12-18 month extension of the sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) blending mandate. The coalition argues that implementing the new rules while jet fuel costs remain elevated creates an untenable operational environment. According to FlightAware tracking data, Jet A-1 fuel costs have doubled from $85 to approximately $170 per barrel since February 2026, directly correlated with heightened Iran tensions disrupting Middle Eastern crude supplies.

The EU's Sustainable Aviation Fuel mandate, initially implemented as part of the 2023 FuelEU Aviation regulations, requires airlines to blend increasing percentages of certified sustainable fuels into their jet fuel supplies—beginning at 2% in 2025 and escalating to 70% by 2050. SAF typically costs 3-5 times more than conventional jet fuel, adding $3,000-$7,000 per flight to operating expenses. With underlying fuel costs already doubling, carriers argue the compounded effect becomes economically unviable without substantial government subsidies or passenger fare increases.

EU officials have not issued formal statements on the delay request, but sources familiar with negotiations indicate Brussels is "carefully evaluating the request" while maintaining public commitment to climate targets. The European Commission's Directorate-General for Mobility and Transport confirmed receiving the petition and stated that "regulatory flexibility is under review in light of extraordinary market conditions." However, no official timeline for a decision has been announced.

The spike impacts all major European hubs. **Paris Charles de Gaulle (CDG)** alone handles 72 million passengers annually; **London Heathrow (LHR)** processes 80 million; **Rome Fiumicino (FCO)** manages 42 million. If airlines proceed with ticket price increases to offset fuel and SAF costs, industry analysts project a 15-22% reduction in European leisure travel demand by Q2 2026, with cascading effects on tourism revenue across Southern Europe.

Next steps include a scheduled April 2, 2026 EU Transport Council meeting where member states will formally debate the mandate delay request. Airlines have committed to submitting detailed cost-impact analyses by March 28, 2026. Industry sources suggest a compromise may emerge: a phased implementation delay to September 2026 or a government subsidy mechanism for SAF purchases.

## Industry Response

Lufthansa Group, Air France-KLM, IAG (International Airlines Group), and Ryanair have united in the delay petition—a rare alignment among traditional and low-cost carriers. This coalition signals that the competitive pressure is existential. Smaller regional carriers and budget operators argue they lack the financial reserves to absorb simultaneous fuel cost and SAF compliance expenses, threatening route profitability across secondary European airports.

Regulatory bodies in other regions are watching closely. The US FAA and ICAO (International Civil Aviation Organization) have not announced comparable SAF mandates, giving North American and Asian carriers competitive advantages if European airlines face unilateral compliance costs. Industry observers predict that if the EU maintains the mandate, European airlines will reduce flight frequency on lower-margin routes, potentially withdrawing service from smaller cities like Nice, Marseille, and regional UK airports in favor of high-density hubs.
